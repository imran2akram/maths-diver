# Mental arithmetic difficulty calibration guide

## Goal
This document is for calibrating a ten-question mental arithmetic game for players aged 6 to 18, while also providing a path to genuinely difficult adult challenge rounds. The aim is to help the game choose questions that are developmentally fair, quick to answer mentally, and progressively harder in a way that feels consistent rather than random. [page:1][page:2][web:6]

## Core design principles
- Treat **fluency** as speed plus accuracy on familiar arithmetic facts, not just eventual correctness. Research on math fluency describes it as solving arithmetic facts quickly and accurately, and shows that fluency keeps developing across the primary years. [web:6]
- Use **secure recall before complexity**. In early primary years, strong number bonds and place value come first; by the end of Year 2 pupils should know number bonds to 20, and by the end of Year 4 pupils should have memorised multiplication tables up to 12 × 12. [page:2]
- Increase difficulty through one variable at a time: larger numbers, more steps, more awkward number structure, decimals/fractions/negatives, or time pressure. This matches school progression frameworks better than abruptly jumping to unfamiliar content. [page:1][page:2]
- For strong players, prefer **richer combinations of known skills** over acceleration to advanced symbolic maths too early. Curriculum guidance recommends challenging rapid learners with richer and more sophisticated problems before pushing them into new content. [page:2]

## What “mental” should mean in the game
A question is suitable for mental arithmetic if a good player can solve it using recall, place value, compensation, partitioning, known facts, or simple multi-step reasoning without needing written working. Typical approved strategies in school progression documents include counting on/back, reordering, bridging through 10 or 100, using known facts, partitioning, compensation, commutativity, associativity, distributive reasoning, and inverse relationships. [page:2]

Good mental questions reward patterns such as `38 + 19 = 38 + 20 - 1`, `25 × 16 = 100 × 4`, or `84 ÷ 6 = (60 ÷ 6) + (24 ÷ 6)`. Poor mental questions are ones that are long, messy, and only hard because they overload working memory. [page:2]

## Recommended age bands
Use age bands as defaults, but allow overlap. Children vary a lot, and official guidance stresses that progression should depend on secure understanding rather than age alone. [page:2]

| Age band | Typical stage | What most players should comfortably manage | What should feel stretching but fair |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6–7 | Early primary | Counting on/back, number bonds to 10 and then 20, single-digit addition/subtraction, doubles, halves, adding/subtracting 1 or 10, simple 2s/5s/10s patterns. [page:2][web:2] | Missing-number bonds, near doubles, teen numbers ± one digit, simple grouped counting, very easy one-step wordless multiplication as repeated addition. [page:2] |
| 8–9 | Lower primary | Addition/subtraction facts to 20, derived facts to 100, two-digit ± one-digit or tens, two two-digit numbers, adding three one-digit numbers, 2/5/10 and then 3/4/8 table facts, simple division facts. [page:2][web:6][web:9] | Bridging through 10/100, compensation like +9 or -9, teen × one digit, two-step mixed fact questions, simple inverse questions. [page:2] |
| 10–11 | Upper primary entry | Secure tables to 12 × 12, three-digit ± ones/tens/hundreds, efficient partitioning, near multiples of 10/100, factor-pair thinking, larger exact divisions, simple decimals and complements. [page:2] | Multi-step arithmetic with reordering, decimals to tenths/hundredths, choosing between equivalent strategies, exact fraction-with-whole-number ideas. [page:1][page:2] |
| 12–13 | Early secondary | Integers, fractions with like/unlike denominators, decimals, percentages, ratio basics, multi-digit operations mentally when structure is friendly. Grade-level fluency tables include add/subtract integers and rational numbers from later middle grades. [page:1] | Fraction-decimal-percent conversion in head, percent change on clean numbers, signed arithmetic, chained operations with traps. [page:1] |
| 14–16 | Mid secondary | Fast arithmetic across whole numbers, decimals, integers, common fractions, percentage multipliers, proportion, square numbers, roots of perfect squares, simple algebraic substitution if you choose to include it. [page:1] | Multi-step percent and ratio reasoning, fractional scaling, scientific notation arithmetic on easy mantissas, estimation-plus-exactness tasks. [page:1] |
| 17–18 | Late secondary | Strong number sense across all school arithmetic, including awkward percentages, index-law-style number handling, exact simplification, and efficient decomposition. [page:1] | Olympiad-lite arithmetic, cleverly structured products/quotients, hidden cancellations, modular-style remainders, and dense multi-step questions solved under time pressure. [page:2] |

## Age-by-age question guidance

### Ages 6–7
At this stage, players are building confidence with counting, number bonds, and simple place value. Questions should feel short, concrete, and pattern-based rather than computationally heavy. [page:2][web:2]

Use mostly:
- `4 + 3`
- `10 - 6`
- `7 + ? = 10`
- `13 + 5`
- `18 - 3`
- `20, 18, 16, ?`
- `Double 6`
- `Half of 10`

Avoid mostly:
- Multiple carries/borrows
- Formal division beyond sharing/grouping intuition
- Fractions beyond half
- Long verbal wording

Target mix for a 10-question set:
- 4 easy fact recall
- 4 one-step number bond / count-on questions
- 2 stretch questions using near doubles or teens

### Ages 8–9
Most players here should be moving from counting-based methods to more fluent recall and simple mental strategies. Good questions reward bridging through 10, place value, and early times-table fluency. [page:2][web:6][web:9]

Use mostly:
- `27 + 6`
- `54 - 20`
- `38 + 9`
- `63 - 8`
- `6 × 4`
- `35 ÷ 5`
- `14 + 8 + 6`
- `46 + 27`

Useful stretch types:
- Missing number: `? + 27 = 50`
- Compensation: `49 + 19`
- Reordering: `8 + 15 + 2`
- Derived facts: `30 ÷ 5` from `3 ÷ ?` patterns

Avoid mostly:
- Heavy decimals
- Unlike-denominator fractions
- Three-step calculations unless very clean

### Ages 10–11
This is the point where table recall should be secure and players can use richer mental strategies with larger whole numbers and some decimals. By the end of Year 4 pupils are expected to know tables to 12 × 12, and later primary progression includes larger numbers, tenths, hundredths, factor pairs, and distributive reasoning. [page:2]

Use mostly:
- `84 ÷ 7`
- `19 × 5`
- `125 + 399`
- `620 - 198`
- `3.4 + 0.8`
- `0.57 + 0.32`
- `25 × 12`
- `48 ÷ 4 + 7`

Good stretch types:
- Near hundreds: `398 + 206`
- Factor tricks: `4 × 25 × 7`
- Decimal complements: `1 - 0.37`
- Choose-the-best-method questions where structure matters

Avoid mostly:
- Ugly decimal places with no mental pattern
- Long chains that become memory tests

### Ages 12–13
Players should now cope with negative numbers, fractions, decimals, percentages, and mixed-operation arithmetic if the numbers are clean. State standards for these grades explicitly include fluent operations with fractions, integers, rational numbers, and decimals. [page:1]

Use mostly:
- `-3 + 8`
- `15% of 80`
- `3/4 + 1/4`
- `1/2 of 36`
- `0.25 × 40`
- `120 ÷ 0.5`
- `2.5 + 3.75`
- `5/6 - 1/3`

Good stretch types:
- Convert forms: `0.125 as a fraction`
- Reverse percent: `A price after 25% off is 60; original price?`
- Signed multi-step arithmetic
- Fraction + percent comparisons

### Ages 14–16
These players can handle genuinely sophisticated arithmetic if the structure is visible. Difficulty should come from efficient reasoning, not from random ugliness. [page:1][page:2]

Use mostly:
- `17.5% of 240`
- `3/8 of 64`
- `99 × 37`
- `125 × 48`
- `1.2 ÷ 0.03`
- `-4^?` style notation should be avoided unless the game teaches order rules explicitly
- `35% of 80 + 15% of 80`
- `sqrt(144) + 15`

Good stretch types:
- Percentage multipliers: increase then decrease
- Ratio scaling: `If 3 items cost 7.50, what do 8 cost?`
- Friendly scientific notation arithmetic
- Hidden simplification in fractions

### Ages 17–18
At this band, most school arithmetic is fair game. High-quality questions should reward elegant decomposition, estimation, cancellation, and choosing the fastest route. [page:1][page:2]

Use mostly:
- `999 × 27`
- `12.5% of 480`
- `48 ÷ 0.125`
- `3/7 + 2/7 of 21` if phrasing is unambiguous
- `2.4 × 25`
- `7.5% of 640`
- `(49 × 51)`
- `1001 ÷ 13`

Good stretch types:
- Remainder logic
- Difference of squares forms
- Multi-step percent and ratio without calculators
- Scientific notation on tidy mantissas

## Adult difficulty scaling
To challenge adults who are good at maths, do **not** merely use bigger numbers. Bigger numbers often just make questions slower, not smarter. Better adult difficulty comes from combining high-fluency arithmetic with structural insight such as compensation, cancellation, factorisation, symmetry, inverses, and hidden benchmarks. This fits school progression guidance that values efficient methods, properties of numbers, and choosing strategies intelligently. [page:2]

### Adult tiers

| Tier | Intended player | Characteristics | Example question types |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adult easy | Numerate adult | Fast school arithmetic, no trick wording | `18% of 50`, `125 × 6`, `84 ÷ 12` |
| Adult medium | Strong everyday numeracy | Two-step reasoning, decimals, percents, ratio | `17.5% of 80`, `0.75 ÷ 0.03`, `15% off 240` |
| Adult hard | Good at maths | Structural shortcuts matter, multiple valid strategies | `49 × 51`, `999 × 64`, `25 × 32 × 125` |
| Adult expert | Very strong mental calculator | Dense but elegant, often one key insight unlocks speed | `9999 × 9999`, `1/16 of 7,200`, `37 × 27 - 37 × 17`, `48 ÷ 0.1875` |

### Reliable ways to make questions harder
1. **Increase structure, not clutter**: `49 × 51` is better than `53 × 47` because it invites a clean method.
2. **Use benchmark numbers**: 25, 50, 75, 125, 250, 0.5, 0.25, 12.5%, 37.5%.
3. **Require strategy choice**: more than one possible route, but one clearly fastest route.
4. **Mix representations**: decimals, fractions, percentages, ratios in one question.
5. **Add one extra mental step**: adults can handle two or three elegant steps.
6. **Use exact cancellation**: e.g. `84 × 25 ÷ 7`.
7. **Use near powers and complements**: 99, 999, 1001, 0.99, 1.001.
8. **Use time pressure carefully**: short time limits amplify difficulty without changing content.

### Bad ways to make questions harder
- More digits with no exploitable pattern
- Awkward primes chosen at random
- Long spoken wording
- Ambiguous order-of-operations questions
- Questions that are mainly working-memory load
- Traps that depend on reading speed rather than number skill

## Suggested difficulty model for the code
Represent each candidate question with a structured difficulty profile rather than one single score.

Recommended fields:
- `age_min`
- `age_max`
- `operation_family` (`add`, `subtract`, `multiply`, `divide`, `mixed`, `fraction`, `decimal`, `percent`, `ratio`, `integer`)
- `facts_required` (for example `bonds_to_10`, `tables_to_12`, `decimal_place_value`)
- `steps_mental` (1, 2, 3, 4)
- `pattern_strength` (`obvious`, `available`, `none`)
- `working_memory_load` (`low`, `medium`, `high`)
- `time_expectation_seconds`
- `difficulty_score`
- `tags` (for example `bridge_10`, `compensation`, `near_multiple`, `factor_pair`, `inverse`, `percent_of_amount`)

A practical rule is that a harder question should usually increase only one of these at a time. For example, do not combine high step count, weak pattern, and high memory load unless the target is expert adults. [page:2]

## Ten-question set templates

### Template for ages 6–7
- 3 fact recall questions
- 3 number-bond questions
- 2 simple teen-number questions
- 1 double/half question
- 1 stretch question

### Template for ages 8–9
- 2 addition/subtraction facts
- 2 place-value questions
- 2 table/division fact questions
- 2 bridge-or-compensation questions
- 2 stretch mixed questions

### Template for ages 10–11
- 2 table/factor questions
- 2 large-number add/subtract questions
- 2 mixed-operation questions
- 2 decimal questions
- 2 stretch strategy questions

### Template for ages 12–16
- 2 decimal/fraction questions
- 2 percent questions
- 2 integer or signed-number questions
- 2 mixed-operation questions
- 2 stretch reasoning questions

### Template for adults
- 2 warm-up fast wins
- 3 medium questions
- 3 hard structured questions
- 2 expert finishers

## Implementation notes for Copilot
- Prefer generating questions from **templates plus constraints**, not raw random numbers.
- For each age band, maintain a whitelist of allowed concept tags and a blacklist of disallowed ones.
- Score questions separately for **concept difficulty**, **number awkwardness**, **steps**, and **working-memory load**.
- Ensure a 10-question set contains variety across operations and strategy types.
- Avoid two very similar questions back-to-back, such as two compensation additions in a row.
- Add telemetry so you can measure answer time, accuracy, and abandon rate by age band and question tag.
- If a player is consistently fast and accurate, first increase question structure and step count, then number awkwardness, and only then introduce new concept families.
- If a player is struggling, reduce steps and memory load before reducing the underlying concept level.

## Simple acceptance criteria
- Age 6 players should mostly see number bonds, counting-on, doubles, and very short one-step arithmetic. [page:2][web:2]
- By roughly age 8 to 9, players should see two-digit add/subtract, early times tables, and simple division facts. [page:2][web:9]
- By roughly age 10 to 11, tables to 12 × 12 and larger-number mental strategies should be expected. [page:2]
- Secondary-age players should increasingly handle decimals, fractions, percentages, integers, and multi-step reasoning. [page:1]
- Adult-hard questions should be selected for elegant structure and strategy demand, not just larger raw numbers. [page:2]

## Suggested prompt for question generation
```text
Generate 10 mental arithmetic questions for target_age = X and target_difficulty = Y.
Constraints:
- Only use concept tags allowed for this age band.
- Keep working_memory_load at or below the target threshold.
- Include at least 3 different strategy types.
- Include 2 easy, 6 on-target, and 2 stretch questions.
- Prefer questions with clean mental methods over messy computation.
- Reject questions that require written working or ambiguous interpretation.
Return JSON with: question, answer, concept_tags, estimated_time_seconds, difficulty_score, explanation_of_fastest_method.
```
